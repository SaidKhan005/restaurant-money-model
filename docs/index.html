1

DEMAND

Attraction

Core Question

"Do I have enough guests to fill my restaurant's capacity?"

Problem Being Solved: Seat demand: enough people, in the right time blocks, choosing us

📊 What You Measure

  • Guest Count
  • Covers by period

💡 What These Metrics Mean

How many people are asking to be served.
WHEN they are asking to be served.

🔑 Key Principle

If seats are empty, operations isn’t the constraint. Demand is.

Earn the right to optimize ops by filling the room.

Deep Dive: Warnings & Levers

⚠️ What does Guest count and Covers Per Period NOT tell You

  • They do not tell you whether your team is efficient
  • They do not tell you whether your kitchen can execute
  • They do not tell you whether the experience holds under pressure

Look at them to measure demand. Nothing else.

✓ Correct Levers
1. Offer First

Great restaurants don’t just serve food, they create moments people look forward to.

Guests are drawn to places that promise an experience worth having. A place where they feel welcomed, taken care of, and confident their time and money will be well spent.

A strong offer is often the first signal of that experience. It tells the guest, before they even arrive, this is going to be worth it.

Start by understanding what your guests hope to feel when they visit, then package that experience so saying yes feels easy and exciting.

To construct an offer, follow this simple framework:

  • Start with the dream outcome
    What experience is the guest hoping to have?
    Examples: quick satisfying lunch, fun night out, family dinner, late-night comfort food
  • Increase the perceived value
    Package the experience so it feels like a great decision.
    Examples: bundles, value meals, limited-time experiences, insider or special items
  • Name the offer
    People remember named offers, not menu descriptions.
    Examples: Burger & Brew, Sunday Supper, Lunch Power Pair, Steak & Sip
  • Remove decision fatigue
    Make it obvious what the guest gets and when it’s available
  • Add scarcity or social proof (bonus)
    Limited availability, popular specials, or guest favorites
2. Then Visibility

Before an offer spreads externally, it must live clearly inside the restaurant.

Great hospitality always starts with the people delivering the experience. If the team understands the offer deeply, it becomes part of how they welcome, guide, and take care of guests.

1. Inside — Align the Team

Your team should understand the offer so well they can explain it without hesitation.

  • They greet guests
  • They recommend dishes
  • They answer questions every day

Their voice is the first and most powerful channel an offer will ever have.

Never create awareness outside the restaurant walls before it exists inside.

When awareness begins externally before it exists internally, it creates friction, confusion, and missed expectations.

Alignment must come first.

2. Insight — Listen and Refine

Restaurants learn the most about their guests through the people who speak with them every day.

Your staff hear feedback in real time. They notice patterns in what guests enjoy, what they ask about, and what they compare you to.

  • They hear feedback
  • They notice patterns
  • They see what other restaurants are doing

This perspective is invaluable.

Before amplifying the offer to the market, allow the team’s insight to refine it.

When the people delivering the experience believe in the offer, every interaction becomes an opportunity to share it.

This shows up in small but powerful moments:

  • Host greetings
  • Server recommendations
  • Menu highlights
  • Table conversations
  • Goodbye scripts

At this stage, the offer begins spreading naturally through conversation.

3. Amplify — Expand Visibility

Once the offer is clearly understood and supported internally, it can begin traveling beyond the restaurant.

Now the message moves outward and reaches the broader community.

Visibility expands through the places where your guests already spend their time and attention.

This may include:

  • Local events
  • Partnerships with schools, businesses, and hotels
  • Paid advertising
  • Warm content
  • Social proof such as reviews, guest photos, and busy dining rooms

Each channel simply carries the same message further.

The goal is not to create new messages.

The goal is to repeat the same offer clearly and consistently.

4. Own — Become Known for It

When an offer is repeated consistently, it becomes familiar.

Guests begin to associate that experience directly with your restaurant.

Clarity creates trust.
Trust creates demand.

Over time, the offer stops feeling like a promotion and starts becoming part of your restaurant’s identity.

The restaurant becomes known for it.

The Simple Version:
Inside → Insight → Amplify → Own
  • Inside — Align the team
  • Insight — Refine through guest feedback
  • Amplify — Expand visibility outside the restaurant
  • Own — Repeat until the market recognizes it
3. Then Access

When guests decide they want the experience you offer, the path to enjoying it should feel effortless.

Access is the bridge between interest and arrival. Once guests are attracted to your offer, the next step is simply making it easy for them to act.

1. Immediate Acknowledgement

Inside the restaurant, access begins at the host stand.

The time between a guest entering, being acknowledged, greeted, and seated should be minimal.

A quick greeting signals attentiveness and hospitality. Even when a wait is unavoidable, immediate acknowledgement reassures guests they have been seen.

Speed and clarity at the front door set the tone for the entire experience.

2. Clear Paths to Act

Outside the restaurant, guests should always have a clear way to respond to your offer.

Every form of promotion or social content should include a visible call-to-action.

Examples include:

  • Reserve now
  • Call to book
  • Walk-ins welcome tonight

Attention without a clear next step creates hesitation. A simple path to act turns curiosity into commitment.

3. Reliable Systems

Access depends on systems that work consistently.

Key operational elements should always be dependable:

  • Hours of operation clearly stated across all channels
  • Reservation systems functioning reliably
  • Phone calls answered promptly and consistently

When these systems fail, guests encounter friction at the exact moment they are ready to act.

Reliability protects momentum.

4. Managed Waiting

Even when demand exceeds capacity, access can still feel welcoming.

Waitlists and text-back systems allow guests to maintain their place while exploring nearby options.

Small gestures can transform waiting from frustration into part of the experience.

Examples include:

  • Offering appetizers while guests wait
  • Providing beverages or bar seating
  • Clear communication about expected wait times

These touches maintain goodwill while preserving guest flow.

The Simple Version:
Access is about removing friction between attraction and arrival.
  • Acknowledge quickly
  • Make action obvious
  • Ensure systems work
  • Manage waiting gracefully
🛡️ Protect the Experience

To reiterate, Great restaurants are remembered for how they make people feel.

When demand begins to slow, it can be tempting to react quickly to protect the bottom line. But some reactions quietly weaken the very experience that attracted guests in the first place.

Two common moves often make the situation worse instead of better.

Cutting Labor
reduces experience capacity → kills conversion → lowers repeat → demand worsens

Cutting staff during or before service may appear to control costs in the moment, but it weakens the restaurant’s ability to deliver the experience guests came for. (More on this in the Experience section.)

Discounting Without a Conversion Path
attracts price buyers → compresses margins → experience weakens → loyal demand declines

Promotions can attract attention, but without a clear path to convert those visits into repeat guests, they become temporary spikes rather than lasting improvements.

Sustainable demand comes from protecting the offer and the experience, not from squeezing labor or chasing short-term traffic.

When demand declines, return to the framework: strengthen the offer, increase visibility, improve access, and protect the experience before cutting labor or discounting.
DEMAND creates the LOAD that tests CAPACITY
Guest attraction creates operational load. Your restaurant's capacity handles that load.
2

CAPACITY

Guest Fulfilment Engine

Core Question

"Can we serve this demand without degrading the guest experience?"

Problem Being Solved: Operational bottleneck

💡 What Capacity Really Means

Capacity is not how many seats we have.

Capacity is how many guests we can take care of
while honoring the standard of experience we’ve committed to creating.

Every formula in this section exists to protect that commitment.

✓ What You Measure

These two numbers tell us whether we can handle what is walking through the door:

  • Guest Capacity
  • Capacity Load (The Go to Metric for Capacity Management)

✓ What Feeds Into These Numbers

Each of these inputs shapes one of the three capacity dimensions we explore below:

  • Covers per Labor Hour
  • Total Labor Hours
  • Average Food Sales per cover
  • Seats
  • Table Turn Time
  • Service Window/period
Deep Dive: How the Capacity System Works

Protecting the Guest Experience in the Rush

Capacity is decided in the rush.

Not across the whole night. Not in the averages. In the busiest 60 to 90 minutes.

Peak Window (PW) = Our busiest continuous 60 to 90 minutes

If we can protect the standard here, we can protect it anywhere.

So what determines Capacity? Three areas of the restaurant, each with its own ceiling.

Three Areas, Three Ceilings

Each area of the restaurant has its own speed limit. The slowest one sets our true capacity.

1 FOH Capacity (Server Driven)

Servers are the engine -- they create covers. Every other FOH role exists to keep that engine running smoothly under pressure.

Server CPLH -- covers per server labor hour during the peak window. Server Labor Hours = Servers x PW hours. Typical range: 5 to 7 in casual dine-in.

Server Hours = PW (in hours) x Number of Servers
FOH Capacity = Server CPLH x Server Hours
Quick Example:

PW = 1.5 hours, 4 servers, Server CPLH = 6

Server Hours = 1.5 x 4 = 6

FOH Capacity = 6 x 6 = 36 covers

Server CPLH Support Ladder

Support roles do not create covers. They protect server throughput so Server CPLH can stay high under load.

No support baseline: about 4 Server CPLH

Host and expo support: about 6 Server CPLH

Strong execution with support: 5 to 7 depending on concept and menu complexity

2) Room Capacity

How many guests the dining room can physically seat and turn during the peak window. This one is usually the easiest to calculate -- it is pure arithmetic.

Room Capacity (covers) = Seats x (PW / Average Turn Time)

PW and Average Turn Time must be in the same unit.

3) Kitchen Capacity

How many covers the kitchen can push out during the peak window. Since we measure kitchen output in dollars (Kitchen SPLH), we need to convert that to covers so all three dimensions speak the same language.

Kitchen SPLH -- Food Sales in PW divided by BOH Labor Hours in PW. Unit: dollars per BOH labor hour. Range: 150 to 250.

Kitchen Food Per Cover -- Food Sales in PW divided by Covers in PW. Unit: dollars per cover.

Kitchen CPLH -- Kitchen SPLH divided by Kitchen Food Per Cover. Unit: covers per BOH labor hour. This converts dollars into a cover count we can compare against the other dimensions.

Kitchen Food Per Cover = Food Sales in PW / Covers in PW
Kitchen CPLH = Kitchen SPLH / Kitchen Food Per Cover
Kitchen Labor Hours = PW (hours) x Kitchen Staff during PW
Kitchen Capacity (covers) = Kitchen CPLH x Kitchen Labor Hours
Our True Guest Capacity

Now we have three ceilings. Which one actually governs our night?

All values are expressed in covers. The lowest value sets the limit.

True Guest Capacity = MIN(Server Capacity, Room Capacity, Kitchen Capacity)

Whichever area is tightest becomes our bottleneck. That is the number we plan around.

Real-Time Signals: Constraint Monitors

The ceiling math tells us our limit on paper. These monitors tell us what is actually happening on the floor, right now. They do not change the ceiling -- they reveal which area is breaking first.

Think of them as dashboard warning lights. When one flashes, you know exactly where to look.

Host Constraint Monitor
  • Metric: Average Turn Time (PW)
  • Standard: your standard turn time
  • Breach: Average Turn Time exceeds standard
Expo Constraint Monitor
  • Metric: Kitchen Chit Time (PW)
  • Standard: your standard kitchen chit time
  • Breach: Kitchen Chit Time exceeds standard
Bar Constraint Monitor
  • Metric: Bar Chit Time (PW)
  • Standard: your standard bar chit time
  • Breach: Bar Chit Time exceeds standard
The One Number We Watch

We now have a ceiling (True Guest Capacity). We know the demand (covers in the peak window). One ratio puts them together:

Capacity Load = Covers in PW / True Guest Capacity

Above 1.0: demand exceeds ceiling. Check the Constraint Monitors to identify which standard is breaching first.

📋 Friday Night: Three Ways It Can Go

Same restaurant. Same peak window. Three different outcomes based on staffing and demand.

Fixed Values:

PW = 1.5 hours, Seats = 80, Average Turn Time = 1.5 hours

Kitchen Staff during PW = 4, Kitchen SPLH = $200, Food Per Cover = $25

Scenario A: 5 Servers (Proper Staffing)

Demand = 40 covers

Server Capacity:

Server Hours = 1.5 x 5 = 7.5

Server Capacity = 6 x 7.5 = 45 covers

Room Capacity:

Room Capacity = 80 x (1.5 / 1.5) = 80 covers

Kitchen Capacity:

Kitchen CPLH = 200 / 25 = 8 covers per labor hour

Kitchen Labor Hours = 1.5 x 4 = 6

Kitchen Capacity = 8 x 6 = 48 covers

True Guest Capacity = MIN(45, 80, 48) = 45 (Server Bottleneck)

Capacity Load = 40 / 45 = 0.89

Constraint Monitor Read:

Host: Average Turn Time vs standard → PASS

Expo: Kitchen Chit Time vs standard → PASS

Bar: Bar Chit Time vs standard → PASS

Now imagine the manager sends one server home to save labor cost. Same demand, one fewer server. Watch what happens to the ceiling.

Scenario B: Manager Cuts to 4 Servers

Demand = 40 covers (unchanged)

Server Hours = 1.5 x 4 = 6

Server Capacity = 6 x 6 = 36 covers

Room Capacity = 80 (unchanged)

Kitchen Capacity = 48 (unchanged)

True Guest Capacity = MIN(36, 80, 48) = 36 (Server Bottleneck)

Capacity Load = 40 / 36 = 1.11 (11% over ceiling)

Constraint Monitor Read:

Host: Average Turn Time vs standard → BREACH

Expo: Kitchen Chit Time vs standard → BREACH

Bar: Bar Chit Time vs standard → PASS

Cutting one server dropped our ceiling from 45 to 36, flipping us from comfortable to overloaded. This is exactly the trap Labor % sets -- it says the cut looks great on paper.

Scenario C: What a Real Saturday Looks Like

Inputs:

PW = 4 hours (240 minutes)

Covers in PW = 155

Food Sales in PW = $3,733.55

Average Turn Time = 50 minutes

Servers = 5, Kitchen Staff = 4, Seats = 80

Server Capacity:

Server Labor Hours = 4 x 5 = 20

Required Server CPLH for this PW = 155 / 20 = 7.75 (slightly above typical 5 to 7 range) Take 7 to be true CPLH because it is the closest integer value to 7.75 we had an expo and host on

Server Capacity = 140 covers

Room Capacity:

Room turns = 240 / 50 = 4.8

Room Capacity = 80 x 4.8 = 384 covers

Kitchen Capacity:

Kitchen Labor Hours = 4 x 4 = 16

Kitchen SPLH standard = 200

Food Per Cover = 3,733.55 / 155 = 24.09

Kitchen CPLH standard = 200 / 24.09 = 8.30

Kitchen Capacity at standard = 8.30 x 16 = 133 covers

True Guest Capacity = MIN(140, 384, 133) = 133

Capacity Load = 155 / 133 = 1.17

Constraint Monitor Read:

Host: Average Turn Time vs standard → BREACH

Expo: Kitchen Chit Time vs standard → BREACH

Bar: Bar Chit Time vs standard → PASS

Note: Load at 1.00 means zero buffer even at required throughput. At standard throughput, load is 1.17, so breach is expected. Any surge amplifies it.

Micro Surge Reality

Capacity Load is calculated across the full PW. Service does not arrive evenly.

Demand arrives in bursts across 5 to 15 minute windows.

Average cover rate = 155 / 240 = 0.65 covers per minute.

If 20 covers land in 10 minutes, instantaneous rate = 2.0 covers per minute. That is about 3x the average rate.

When instantaneous demand exceeds sustainable throughput, kitchen chit time rises and breaches standard even when PW load reads 1.00 against required throughput.

This is why Constraint Monitors matter. They reveal breach during bursts when the ceiling math still reads 1.00.

🔴 High Capacity Load vs Low Capacity Load

High Capacity Load (> 0.95)

Operating at or beyond True Guest Capacity

What it looks like on paper:
  • Covers/LH ↑
  • Labor % ↓
  • Table turns faster
What it feels like on the floor:
  • Servers stop suggesting
  • Guests stop asking
  • Drinks don’t get refilled
  • Decisions get rushed
What actually breaks:
  • Avg check
  • Beverage attach
  • Dessert rate
  • Return visits
Why it’s deceptive:

Revenue often holds temporarily because volume masks decay. This is why restaurants feel "busy but broke."

Low Capacity Load (< 0.70)

Operating well below True Guest Capacity

What it looks like on paper:
  • Covers/LH ↓
  • Labor % ↑
  • Low throughput
What actually breaks:
  • Underutilized labor
  • Idle fixed costs
  • Weak contribution margin
  • Excess managerial energy with no throughput
The Problem:

You’re paying for capacity you’re not using. Fixed costs get spread over fewer guests, killing unit economics.

Note:

Low Capacity Load also creates behavioral problems (dead room, no energy). See BEHAVIOR section for how this affects spending psychology.

🎯 Where We Want to Land: The Optimal Zone

Optimal Capacity Load Range: 0.75 to 0.90

Operating at 75-90% of True Guest Capacity

In this zone:

True Guest Capacity isn’t maxed out
Capacity Load leaves room for variability
Experience standards are preserved
Staff energy remains stable
Contribution margin improves
Operational consistency is maintained
This is where:
  • Guests feel seen
  • Servers can guide decisions
  • The room has energy calibrated to intended behavior
Energy Calibration:

See BEHAVIOR section for detailed explanation of energy alignment and psychological drivers.

  • For turnover models: Social, upbeat, momentum toward next round
  • For dwell models: Intimate, relaxed, momentum toward lingering
  • Energy mismatches: Clubbers among diners → diners leave early

🔑 The Principle Behind All of This

True Guest Capacity defines your ceiling.

Capacity Load reveals whether you’re operating within it.

The goal is NOT more labor or less labor.

The goal is: Keep Capacity Load in the optimal zone.

So if the goal is not to chase Labor %, what is Labor % actually telling us?

❌ Why Chasing Labor % Can Hurt You

Here is the trap: Labor % improves when you do these three things --

  • You cut attention
  • You remove slack
  • You reduce optionality

All three REDUCE True Guest Capacity or INCREASE Capacity Load beyond optimal.

Labor % ignores whether Capacity Load is sustainable.

Instead of cutting to improve a percentage, here is how to actually raise the ceiling.

✓ How to Raise Your Ceiling (Without Burning Your Team)

These structural changes increase your ceiling without degrading experience:

  • Section Geometry: Give each server fewer tables during the rush so they can give each guest more attention = higher covers per labor hour
  • Temporal Staggering: Overlap server shifts so you peak with more hands, not the same hands stretched thinner = better load distribution
  • Door Control: Use reservation pacing so demand arrives at a rate your team can actually handle = predictable capacity load
  • Role Specialization: Let each person do one thing well instead of asking everyone to do everything = higher throughput per server

Each increases True Guest Capacity while keeping Capacity Load manageable.

📊 How to Tell If You Are in the Zone (Without the Formula)

If you cannot calculate Capacity Load directly, these three guest-facing signals will tell you whether your team has room to breathe:

  • Average Check: Drops when guests feel rushed
  • Beverage Attach Rate: Drops when servers lack attention
  • Dessert Rate: Drops when turns are forced
CAPACITY sets the stage for EXPERIENCE delivery and BEHAVIOR psychology
Capacity load affects both operational delivery (experience) and guest buying psychology (behavior).
3

EXPERIENCE

Value Creation Engine

Core Question

"Given the time and attention the guest gave us, did we turn it into value worth repeating?"

Problem Being Solved: LONG TERM VALUE (LTV) conversion constraint

📊 Experience Integrity Indicators

Experience does not show up as a single metric. It reveals itself through outcome patterns.

  • Turn Time → Is delivery paced in a way that protects value?
  • Complaints / Comps → Where did execution break down?
  • Repeat Visit Rate → Did operational delivery earn trust?

These indicators remain healthy when Capacity Load stays within the optimal range (0.75 to 0.90). When they decline, the root cause is often upstream. Check CAPACITY before adjusting training, scripts, or pricing.

💡 Reframing

Experience is NOT simply "exceptional service quality."

Experience is the operational delivery of value AND converting a guest's time and attention into something worth repeating.

💡 Operational Excellence

Experience is created through operations.

When delivery is consistent and friction is quietly removed, the visit feels effortless to the guest.

Excellence lives in the system behind the scenes.

Deep Dive: Value Equations, Killers & LTV

🎯 The Operational Value Equation

Experience is operational value delivered under constraint. This equation defines what we control. See BEHAVIOR for how guests perceive this value psychologically.

Operational Value = (What We Deliver × Consistency) ÷ (Speed × Friction)
🟢 What We Deliver

Operationally: The tangible product and service the guest receives.

Floor signals:

  • Food temp off, portions inconsistent
  • Refills missed, modifications wrong

Moves: Average check, comp rate, repeat visit rate

Fix first: Audit plate quality and order accuracy at the pass

🟢 Consistency

Operationally: Delivering the same standard across every shift and every visit.

Floor signals:

  • Tuesday quality differs from Friday
  • New staff execute differently
  • Kitchen timing varies by shift

Moves: Repeat visit rate, complaint frequency, staff-level avg check variance

Fix first: Standardize prep, ticket flow, and service sequence

🟢 Speed

Operationally: The pacing of delivery relative to guest expectations.

Floor signals:

  • Ticket times stretching
  • Drinks arriving after food
  • Check delayed at end of meal

Moves: Turn time, beverage attach (second round dies when speed lags), dessert rate

Fix first: Track ticket time by station and identify the bottleneck

🟢 Friction

Operationally: Any operational failure that interrupts the guest's experience.

Floor signals:

  • POS goes down, item 86'd mid-order
  • Payment takes too long
  • Staff unsure of process

Moves: Comp rate, average check, repeat visit rate

Fix first: Eliminate the most frequent system failure first

When delivery is high, consistency is high, and friction is low, the equation produces value. BEHAVIOR monetizes that value downstream.

🔴 Value Killers

1. Cognitive Overload
Symptoms:
  • Many menu items
  • No recommendations
  • Guests ask "what's good?" repeatedly
  • Avg items per guest ↓ even with time
Metrics that reveal it:
  • High menu size + low items/guest
  • Longer ordering time + fewer add-ons
Fix:
  • Server-led curation
  • Bundles
  • Anchors
2. Attention Dilution
Symptoms:
  • Server technically present, but distracted
  • Long gaps between touches
  • Guests don't order second drinks
Metrics:
  • Beverages per order ↓
  • Second-round drinks drop specifically
  • Stable covers, lower drink attach
Fix:
  • Reduce section size
  • Add drink-check prompts
  • Rebalance FOH load
4. Friction with Staff
Symptoms:
  • Mechanical service
  • No warmth
  • Guests feel processed, not hosted
Metrics:
  • Complaints without operational errors
  • Low tip % on certain shifts
  • Avg check lower on specific servers
Fix:
  • Coaching tone, not speed
  • Hospitality-first training
5. Rushed Experience

Root Cause: Capacity overload

Symptoms:
  • Guests feel hurried
  • No time for second drinks
  • Decisions feel pressured
  • No moments or storytelling
Metrics:
  • High Covers/LH + Low Avg Check
  • Fast table turns + Low beverage attach
  • Low dessert rate despite time
Fix:
  • This cannot be fixed with training or scripts alone
  • PRIMARY: GO BACK TO CAPACITY LAYER
  • Reduce sections, control door, add staggered labor
  • THEN layer in training/scripts once capacity is balanced

🚫 Why Lowering Prices & Making Items Cheaper Kills Value

Lowering prices damages Dream Outcome AND Perceived Likelihood simultaneously.

When you discount or cheapen items, you signal: "This isn't worth what we're charging."

This permanently weakens value perception. Guests remember the discount, not the experience.

📋 Example: Same Peak Window, Two Outcomes

Covers in Peak Window: 110 (identical both nights)

Equation Strong Night

Avg Check: $44

Beverage Attach: 1.25

Dessert Rate: 18%

Comps: 1%

30-Day Repeat Rate: 22%

Equation Weak Night

Avg Check: $39

Beverage Attach: 0.95

Dessert Rate: 10%

Comps: 3.5%

30-Day Repeat Rate: 16%

What changed operationally:

  • Ticket times stretched (12 → 18 minutes)
  • Touch points dropped (3 → 2 per table)
  • Checkout friction added +3 minutes
  • One POS delay + one 86 created cascading friction
Same volume. Different value conversion.

📋 How the Equation Affects Metrics

Equation Strong (Operational Reality)

  • Ticket times stable
  • Touch points maintained
  • Checkout friction low

→ Beverage attach holds → Dessert rate holds → Avg check holds

Equation Weak (Operational Reality)

  • Ticket times stretch
  • Touch points drop
  • Checkout stalls

→ Beverage attach drops first → Dessert rate drops next → Avg check softens last

📊 When Value Erodes, These Metrics Move

  • Beverage Attach Rate ↓
  • Dessert Rate ↓
  • Average Check ↓
  • Repeat Visit Rate ↓

Operational value does not end at the check. When delivery is consistent and friction is removed, value compounds over time.

💰 LTV: Lifetime Value (The Operational Foundation)

When the value equation is strong, trust builds. When trust builds, guests return. When guests return, Lifetime Value compounds.

LTV = Avg Spend × Visit Frequency × Retention Period
Example:

Avg spend: $45

Visits/year: 6

Years retained: 4

LTV = $1,080

One Bad Operational Experience Can Kill 4 Years of Revenue

If retention period drops from 4 years to 0 because of a single operational failure:

Lost LTV: $1,080

Without reliable operational delivery, even strong behavioral framing (discussed below) cannot sustain long-term retention.
EXPERIENCE delivers value, BEHAVIOR converts it to money
Experience creates operational value (product delivered well). Behavior converts that value into psychological response (spending, returning, upselling). You can't monetize bad experience, but good experience doesn't monetize itself.

BEHAVIOR

Framing & Psychology Engine

Core Question

"Are guests being guided to higher-value decisions?"

Problem Being Solved: Sales execution through framing

📊 What You Measure

  • Avg Guest Check
  • Items per Guest
  • Dessert Rate
  • Beverage Attach Rate

Slice by: Server (reveals framing effectiveness) • Daypart (reveals energy alignment) • Capacity Load (reveals psychological conditions)

Note: These measure psychological response and spending conversion.

💡 The Core Insight

Same menu. Same prices. Different outcome.

That means framing, not economics.

People do not resist price. They resist unexplained price.
Deep Dive: Psychology, Framing & Monetization

⚡ How Capacity Load Affects Behavior

Capacity load determines whether the room can create the psychological conditions for spending behavior:

When Capacity Load Is Too HIGH (> 0.95)
  • Servers lack attention → Can't frame or guide decisions
  • Rushed service → Guests self-limit spending
  • No time for recommendations → Upsells don't happen
  • Friction increases → Behavioral resistance rises
Behavioral Impact: Avg check drops, attach rates fall, guests pay and leave. Same experience delivered, different behavioral response.
When Capacity Load Is Too LOW (< 0.70)
  • Energy Alignment Breaks: Room feels dead, no momentum toward intended behavior (turnover models lose upbeat energy, dwell models feel empty)
  • Social Reinforcement Breaks: Guests don't see behavioral cues from other tables (no one ordering drinks, no one lingering)
  • Behavioral Momentum Breaks: No natural flow pushing guests toward the intended spending patterns
  • Psychological Status Drops: "Did I pick the wrong place?" feeling emerges, spending hesitation increases
Key Truth: A half-empty room lacks the social proof and energy direction that drives intended behavior. Guests consume more and stay longer in rooms with the right energetic alignment.
Behavioral Impact: Lower spend per guest, faster exits, reduced repeat likelihood. The experience was delivered fine, but behavioral psychology broke.
When Capacity Load Is OPTIMAL (0.75-0.90)
  • Servers have attention to guide behavior and frame choices
  • Room has energy calibrated to your model (social for turnover, intimate for dwell)
  • Social reinforcement works (guests see others ordering drinks, staying longer)
  • Behavioral momentum pulls guests toward intended spending patterns
  • Energy alignment creates psychological conditions for higher spend
Behavioral Impact: Framing works, upsells convert, guests don't resist price, repeat rates climb. This is where behavior monetizes experience.

🧠 Psychological Drivers of Spending Behavior

Beyond operational delivery, these psychological factors drive whether guests spend more and return:

Social Status

Guests spend more when the choice signals something positive about them to others. "Being seen here" or "ordering this" becomes part of identity.

Behavioral lever: Scarcity, curation, chef recommendations, exclusivity.

Psychological Status (Feeling Good About the Choice)

Guests need to feel they made the "right" decision. Regret kills repeat visits. Confidence in choice drives higher spend and return likelihood.

Behavioral lever: Social proof, expert curation, removing decision fatigue.

Energy Alignment (Room Matches Intended Behavior)

Room energy creates behavioral momentum. Upbeat energy → guests order more rounds. Intimate energy → guests linger and order extras. Mismatched energy → spending suppression.

Behavioral lever: Capacity load optimization, music, lighting, pacing, social density.

These are BEHAVIORAL outcomes, not operational outputs.

Experience delivers the product. Behavior creates the psychology that drives spending.

🎯 The Psychological Value Equation

This is HOW guests perceive the value we delivered. See EXPERIENCE for the operational equation (what we actually deliver).

Perceived Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)
🟢 Dream Outcome (Psychological)
  • Feeling taken care of
  • Feeling "in on something"
  • Feeling relaxed and indulged
  • Social status (being seen here matters)
  • Psychological status (feeling good about the choice)
  • Identity alignment ("this is my kind of place")
🟢 Perceived Likelihood (Trust)
  • Confident server recommendations (authority)
  • Social proof (others ordering same thing)
  • Brand reputation (past experiences)
  • Visible signals of competence (clean kitchen, organized service)
  • Menu curation (fewer choices = more trust)
🟢 Time Delay (Psychological Waiting Cost)
  • Patience threshold (how long they'll wait)
  • Immediate gratification need
  • Early acknowledgment (reduces perceived wait)
  • Progress indicators (apps arriving builds momentum)
🟢 Effort & Sacrifice (Psychological Friction)
  • Price perception (not actual price, how it feels)
  • Decision fatigue (too many choices)
  • Menu decoding effort (confusing descriptions)
  • Regret risk ("what if I order wrong?")
Same Product, Different Perception

Two guests get the SAME operational experience. One perceives high value (framed well), one perceives low value (not framed).

This is the power of behavioral framing.

How to Use This Equation:

Every behavioral technique in this section manipulates one or more variables in this equation:

  • Curated pairings → Increase Dream Outcome + reduce Effort
  • Chef recommendations → Increase Perceived Likelihood
  • Early appetizers → Reduce Time Delay perception
  • Anchoring → Reduce Effort & Sacrifice (price feels better)

🧠 Making Higher Prices Feel Like a Deal

A price feels like a deal when at least two of these are true:

  • It removes effort or risk

  • Intentional

Same price. Higher uptake.

Concrete Example: Drinks

Bad:

Good:

Higher price. Higher perceived value.

ECONOMICS & COST REALITY

Prime Cost Engineering & Validation

Core Question

"Which items produce the most profit per constraint, and did our menu decisions increase profit density?"

This is prime cost engineering at item level + validation at aggregate level

💡 Reframing Economics

Economics is NOT "food cost %."

Economics is prime cost engineering: understanding which items consume the most constraint and produce the most profit.

🎯 What This Layer Does

Two-part system:

  • Engineering: Which items to push/fix/delete
  • Validation: Did our decisions work?
Deep Dive: Prime Cost Engineering & Validation

🔢 Prime Cost Defined

Prime Cost (item-level):

Prime Cost = Ingredient Cost + Labor Cost to produce and serve

Prime Cost % = Prime Cost ÷ Sell Price

Ingredient Cost:

Recipe costed (including sub-recipes, yields, waste assumptions)

Labor Cost:

Time-on-task × loaded wage rate (prep + line + plating)

Why We Use Prime Cost:

Because the two biggest controllable cost buckets in restaurants are COGS and labor. Prime cost is literally those combined.

When an item is "high volume" but "bad economics," it's either:

  • Sucking labor minutes, or
  • Eating food cost, or
  • Both... and volume amplifies the damage

💰 The Blunt Comparison

✓ Example 1: Chips + Guac + Salsa

Menu price: $17.00

Assumptions per order:

Ingredient cost (COGS): $4.50

  • Avocados + mix-ins
  • Salsa ingredients
  • Corn/oil/salt portion

Labor minutes: 3.5 min

  • Plating/scooping: ~0.5
  • Chips fry + season: ~1.5
  • Guac/salsa prep (batch): ~1.5
Prime Cost Math:

Labor cost: 3.5 × $0.35 = $1.23

✅ Prime cost: $4.50 + $1.23 = $5.73

✅ Prime cost %: $5.73 ÷ $17.00 = 33.7%

What this means:

Even "house-made" can be a profit-dense hero if prep is batched and portioned tightly. The danger is when prep time isn't controlled and silently balloons.

❌ Example 2: Taquitos

Menu price: $14.50

Assumptions per order:

Ingredient cost (COGS): $3.80

  • Tortillas + filling
  • Oil absorption
  • Garnish/sauce

Labor minutes: 9.5 min

  • Portion filling: ~1.5
  • Roll taquitos: ~3.0
  • Fry time + handling: ~3.5
  • Plate + garnish: ~1.5
Prime Cost Math:

Labor cost: 9.5 × $0.35 = $3.33

❌ Prime cost: $3.80 + $3.33 = $7.13

❌ Prime cost %: $7.13 ÷ $14.50 = 49.1%

What this means:

This is the classic high-volume economics trap: the food cost looks fine, but the minutes are expensive, and at volume it also eats fryer bandwidth (a real bottleneck), which can drag the whole kitchen.

The Blunt Comparison:

Chips/Guac/Salsa: ~34% prime cost (good)

Taquitos: ~49% prime cost (heavy)

A bleeder can still be strategic if it reliably pulls guests in AND you have a high-attach path (drinks, starters, desserts). That's money model logic: "Traffic product" that monetizes downstream.

🔧 Quadrant B: How to Engineer a Bleeder

High volume + Bad prime cost = Don't delete yet. Fix it first:

1. Reduce labor minutes
  • Pre-prep components
  • Simplify build
  • Reduce modifiers
  • Station redesign
2. Change the form, not the item

Turn it into a bundle base that forces add-ons with low incremental labor.

Example: "burger + fries" becomes "burger set" where the profitable side is default and labor-heavy variation is optional/paid.

3. Raise price without triggering resistance
  • Reframe (see Behavior step)
  • Anchor with premium version first
4. Cap demand intentionally
  • Limited availability
  • Daypart-only (lunch special)
  • "While supplies last"

If it's a bleeder, you don't want it unlimited.

🎯 The Action (Chips vs Taquitos)

Your action isn't "promote chips & guac because margin."

Your action is:

"Make chips & guac the default first course path, and de-volume taquitos or rebuild them to cut minutes."

That's prime cost engineering.

🧠 The Profit Engineering Move

Stop thinking: "What should we promote?"

Start thinking: "What should we make the default choice?"

Defaults win because humans hate effort.

Make the low-prime-cost items the default:

  • "Our most popular starter is the chips & guac" (not "would you like a starter?")
  • "This comes with our house sides" (not "which sides?")
  • "Most tables start with the sharing platter" (not "small or large?")

VALIDATION: Did Your Menu Decisions Work?

Now we zoom out from item-level to aggregate-level

📊 Aggregate Prime Cost

Formula:

Prime Cost = COGS + Total Labor Costs

Prime Cost % = Prime Cost ÷ Sales

Common Benchmark:

55-65% depending on concept

Full service often higher than QSR

What Prime Cost Validates:
  • Did menu engineering reduce constraint consumption?
  • Did capacity fixes increase throughput?
  • Did behavior changes increase spend/hour?

🌉 SPLH: The Bridge Metric

Sales per Labor Hour:

SPLH = Total Sales ÷ Total Labor Hours

Why This Matters:

SPLH is the bridge between "ops reality" and "financial reality."

It forces the right question:

"Did the same labor hour produce more output?"

🎛️ Break Prime Cost Into Controllable Levers

A) COGS Control
  • Waste + comps/voids
  • Portion control
  • Vendor pricing
  • Prep yield loss
B) Labor Control
  • Scheduling match to demand
  • Deployment (sections, runners, expo)
  • Skill (training reduces rework)

🎯 Good Win vs Bad Win

❌ Bad Win: Fake Improvement
What you did:
  • Cut 1 server
What looks good on paper:
  • SPLH rises (labor hours drop)
  • Prime cost % improves
What actually happened:
  • Beverage attach drops
  • Desserts disappear
  • Reviews dip
Verdict:

You raised a ratio by breaking conversion. This is a fake win.

✓ Good Win: Real Improvement
What you did:
  • Same labor hours
  • Better pacing + better prompts
  • Engineered menu toward low-prime-cost items
What happened:
  • Avg check rises
  • Low-prime-cost items become defaults
Result:
  • Prime cost % improves
  • SPLH rises
Verdict:

Prime cost improved because profit density increased, not because you starved the floor. This is a real win.

🚨 The "Don't Lie to Yourself" Test

If SPLH rises but experience signals fall, you didn't improve. You squeezed.

Watch these together:

  • SPLH (output per hour)
  • Prime cost % (are we engineering better?)
  • Experience signals (beverage attach, dessert attach, comps/voids, complaints)
If SPLH ↑ but beverage attach ↓ = You broke conversion to improve a ratio

⚠️ The Cardinal Sin

Never cut labor before fixing:

  • Conversion
  • Framing
  • Throughput
  • Menu engineering

Cutting labor before fixing conversion = shrinking both today and tomorrow.

🔑 Prime Cost is a Scoreboard, Not a Lever

Prime cost answers: "Did the previous fixes increase profit density?"

It does NOT answer:

  • What to cut
  • Who to blame
Only then do you tune labor.

🧠 Psychological Tie-In

Low-prime-cost items should also be:

  • Easy to explain
  • Easy to pair
  • Easy to recommend
If it's profitable but awkward to sell, it won't move.

Deep Dive: Profit Lenses, Cascades & Compounding

🎯 Three Profit Lenses

You need profit measures that match how restaurants actually live:

1. Net Profit Margin

The "after everything" truth

Restaurants often sit in the 3-5% range, though it varies widely by model.

This is what's left after all expenses.

2. Restaurant-Level Profit

Ops controllable contribution

What the store produces before corporate overhead.

This is what operators can actually control.

3. Profit per Labor Hour

The killer metric

Formula:

Profit per Labor Hour = Sales − (Prime Cost per hour)

Where Prime Cost = COGS + Labor for that hour

This is unit economics for restaurants.

This is how you stop worshipping sales.

🚨 Why "Busy" Can Still Be Broke

Profit is what's left after you:

  • Served
  • Staffed
  • Paid for product
  • Didn't torch the guest experience
"Busy" without profit = breaking even at high volume

You're working harder to make the same (or less) money.

Profit compounds when:

Demand is strong → Seats are full without discounting
Capacity is balanced → Guests feel seen, servers can sell
Experience converts → Guests say yes to upsells and return
Behavior monetizes → Higher checks without more labor
Economics engineer profit density → Low-prime-cost items become defaults
Then profit becomes inevitable.
